Before You Go
Government Registration & Documents
Action Required Spain is currently rated Level 2: Exercise Increased Caution by the U.S. State Department. Register your trip before you leave. It's free, takes 5 minutes, and means the Embassy can reach you in an emergency.

The Smart Traveler Enrollment Program (STEP) is a free service from the U.S. Department of State that lets you register your trip with the nearest embassy. In a natural disaster, civil emergency, or family crisis, they can contact you — or your emergency contacts can locate you through them. No downside to signing up.

Culture & Etiquette
How not to be that American

Mallorca is part of Spain — and while it's one of Europe's most-visited tourist islands, locals have deeply held social customs that differ from American expectations. The biggest shift is slowing down and tuning in. Spaniards live life at a more intentional pace and can spot entitlement from a mile away. Good news: a little effort goes a very long way.

✓ Do
  • Say "Hola" when entering any shop or restaurant and "Gracias" when leaving
  • Greet people warmly — locals appreciate a smile and basic courtesy
  • Match the volume of the room — Spaniards are expressive but not shouters in public
  • Be patient at restaurants — service is intentionally unhurried and that's the point
  • Ask for the check when you're ready — it won't come unless you request it
  • Respect siesta hours (roughly 2–5pm) — some shops close, things slow down
  • Learn a few Spanish phrases — even botching them earns genuine warmth
  • Accept the pace of things: meals are social events, not fuel stops
  • Use two fingers in a café to order two coffees — minimal pointing is fine here
✕ Don't
  • Talk loudly in public, restaurants, or at your hotel — it's the #1 American tell
  • Demand things or snap your fingers at waitstaff — genuinely considered rude
  • Expect 24/7 service culture — Spain does not operate on American convenience schedules
  • Show up to dinner at 6 or 7pm — you'll be eating alone; locals eat at 9pm+
  • Wear swimwear or beachwear into restaurants, shops, or off the beach area
  • Take horse-drawn carriage rides — horses often suffer in summer heat
  • Fly a drone over residential areas or private land without checking local rules
  • Assume everyone speaks English — attempt Spanish first, even badly
  • Tip aggressively — it can read as condescending, not generous
The Greeting Thing When meeting someone socially in Spain, expect two air kisses — right cheek first. Women do it with everyone; men typically shake hands with other men. Don't panic — if you see it coming, just lean in. It's a warmth thing, not a boundary violation.

Dress Code
How to look like you belong

Spaniards interpret "casual" very differently than Americans. The key principle: put-together, not fancy. You don't need to look like you're going to a wedding — but cargo shorts and a sports jersey will absolutely clock you as a tourist.

🏖️ Beach

Swimwear is appropriate at the beach and pool only. Wrap up with a cover-up and sandals the moment you head toward the promenade, restaurant, or shops.

🍽️ Dining Out

Casual linen shirt or blouse, light trousers or a midi dress. Most resort restaurants require long trousers and closed shoes — no flip flops, no tank tops.

🏛️ Palma Old Town

You'll walk cobblestones and enter the Cathedral — have shoulders covered. A light scarf doubles as both a church cover and a stylish accessory.

🌆 Day Exploring

Neat shorts (not cargo) or chinos, a fitted t-shirt or button-down. Comfortable sneakers. Think "Barcelona brunch" not "theme park."

✓ Good Choices
  • Linen shirts, light cotton blouses
  • Fitted chinos or lightweight trousers
  • Midi dresses, sundresses with straps
  • Clean leather sandals or loafers
  • Simple structured tote or crossbody bag
  • Sunglasses that aren't aggressively branded
✕ Avoid
  • Cargo shorts, board shorts off the beach
  • Sports jerseys or loud branded gear
  • Flip flops away from the beach
  • Tank tops in restaurants or shops
  • Fanny packs worn visibly (crossbody = fine)
  • Baseball caps indoors or in restaurants

Dining Rules
Meal times, ordering, and local food culture

Dining in Spain is a fundamentally different experience than the U.S. It is slow, social, and structured around specific times. Adapt to it and it's one of the best parts of the trip. Fight it and you'll be frustrated.

⏰ Meal Times

Breakfast: 8–10am. Light — coffee and a pastry.
Lunch: 2–4pm. The main meal of the day.
Dinner: 9–11pm. Locals eat late; tourist spots open at 7pm.

🧾 Getting the Check

Your waiter will not bring the check until you ask. Say "La cuenta, por favor" — this is not rude, it's just how it works.

🍞 Free Snacks

Aioli, olives, and bread are often brought before your meal — usually free or included. Don't be surprised, just enjoy.

🥘 Eat Local

Try pa amb oli (bread with olive oil and tomato), tumbet (vegetable casserole), and fresh seafood. These are distinctly Mallorcan — don't leave without trying them.

Siesta Warning Some smaller restaurants close between ~2pm and 5pm even if their sign says otherwise. If you get hungry mid-afternoon, your hotel buffet is your best friend. Plan your meals around Spanish times and you won't hit this wall.

Many restaurants close on Sundays and Mondays — not just in villages but even in popular areas. If you're planning a specific dinner, check ahead or have a backup. This is especially true if you take a day trip out of Playa de Palma into smaller Mallorcan towns.

Tipping Guide
Modest, optional, always in cash

Tipping in Spain is not mandatory and is never expected the way it is in the US. Spanish service workers earn a real wage — they don't depend on tips to pay rent. A tip is purely a "thank you for great service," not an obligation. Overtipping can actually feel a bit awkward to locals. When in doubt: round up, leave a few coins, and you're golden.

Key Phrases
Spanish — Mallorca also uses Mallorquí (a Catalan dialect)

English is widely understood in the resort and tourist areas of Playa de Palma. But making the effort to use even basic Spanish is noticed and appreciated — and genuinely opens doors. You'll be greeted with much more warmth if you try. Locals speak both Spanish (Castellano) and Mallorquí — don't be confused by bilingual street signs.

Getting Around
Taxis, buses, and what to know about Uber
No Uber in Mallorca Uber and Lyft are not available in Mallorca or the rest of Spain (Cabify operates in some mainland cities, but not reliably on the island). Use metered taxis — they're readily available and line up at the airport arrivals area.
✈️ Airport → Hotel

Taxis are lined up at Palma Airport (PMI). The ride to Playa de Palma takes about 10–15 minutes and costs roughly €15–22. Agree on the fare or confirm the meter is running.

🚌 Local Bus

Bus stops run directly outside most hotels to Palma city center — about 15–20 min for €2 flat fare. EMT Palma is the city operator. Easy and cheap for day trips into Old Town.

🚶 On Foot

Playa de Palma is walkable along the beachfront promenade. Getting around between Balneario points (beach sections) is totally doable on foot or by renting a bike from your hotel.

🚗 Renting a Car

If you want to explore the island beyond Palma, renting a car unlocks everything. Scenic mountain roads, hidden coves, small villages. International Driver's License recommended but not always required.

Always carry some cash for taxis — while most registered cabs take cards, smaller operators may not. If using a taxi, you can always say "quédese con el cambio" to leave the change as a tip when paying cash. Have your hotel address written down or saved in your phone to show drivers.

Safety & Smart Habits
Common sense + Spain-specific stuff

Mallorca and Palma are very safe for tourists overall — especially at beach resorts. The main risks are pickpocketing in crowded areas, tourist scams, and the kind of trouble that comes from drinking in unfamiliar party zones. Use common sense and you'll be fine.

✓ Smart Habits
  • Keep your bag in front of you in crowded markets and promenades
  • Use a hotel safe for passports, extra cash, and cards you don't need
  • Only carry the cash and cards you need for the day
  • Photograph the menu or note the restaurant's address before you enter
  • Be skeptical of anyone approaching you unsolicited to "help"
  • Save emergency numbers in your phone before arriving
✕ Avoid
  • Leaving bags or valuables unattended at the beach (even briefly)
  • Wearing flashy jewelry or showing expensive gear in busy areas
  • Accepting drinks from strangers you don't know in bar areas
  • Trusting unofficial "tour guides" who approach you on the street
  • Giving money to anyone claiming to be a U.S. military person in trouble (scam)
  • Walking alone late at night in poorly lit or unfamiliar areas
